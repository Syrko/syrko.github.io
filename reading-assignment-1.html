Enhancing Our Lives with immersive Virtual Reality

The article “Enhancing Our Lives with immersive Virtual Reality” by Mel Slater and Maria V. Sanchez-Vives, is a comprehensive overview of the different approaches and applications of Virtual Reality (VR) in a multitude of fields.

A safe conclusion to draw from the article, is the importance placed in achieving the immersion level known as “presence” in VR applications, the illusion of “being there” combined with the “plausibility” that events are really happening. A result of this would be the participants of a VR experience suspending their disbelief and behaving as they would in the physical reality. It transforms the VR application into a tool that could be used to set up valuable experiments that otherwise would not be ethical or even achievable through traditional means. It allows the implementation of training scenarios that will hone skills, transferable to the physical reality, and will prepare the participants for similar real-life situations. VR applications through the “presence” and the “body-ownership” concepts, can facilitate the introspection of human (and not only that) behaviour, as well as provide entertainment through games and virtual travelling.

The avenues for VR exploration and application that have already been opened and will continue to do so are countless. Nevertheless, most of the above applications of VR are difficult to evaluate, and as mentioned in the article they “should be evidence-based”, and “scientific papers should adhere to the highest standards of rigor and reporting”.

Finally, the concepts explored by the article are getting more and more topical by the day as technology advances, the price of equipment falls and the appetite of the scientific and the general public for VR increases. The obstacles to the advancement of VR are shrinking, while the community is waiting for the paradigm shifts that will enable new developments and the theorized change of the world.

Would You Give a Virtual Electric Shock to an Avatar?

In this blog   we have an example of a virtual reality (VR) application that allowed for a variation of Stanley Milgram’s controversial experiment on obedience to authority to be carried out, even though the original experiment is not considered ethical by modern standards.

By utilising the immersion offered by the VR experience, the researchers recreated the experiment ethically, using an avatar, with the assumption that the participants would act, to a degree, as they would in the real world.

It allowed the researchers to observe the behaviour of the participants, measure their stress levels, and to draw conclusions on the psychological cost for those who resisted the authority, as in the original experiment.
